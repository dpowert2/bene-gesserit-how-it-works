System Architecture

How Bene Gesserit works

An AI-powered startup intelligence system that learns what you're looking for — and gets sharper with every decision you make.

01 — The Problem

Startup scouting doesn't learn

Corporate innovation teams scan hundreds of startups a week. They use spreadsheets, Google Alerts, analyst reports, and gut feel. When they pass on a company, the reasoning disappears. When they like something, the pattern is never captured. Next week, they start from scratch.

The result is a process that is expensive, inconsistent, and — most critically — static. A human analyst gets better over time, but the system around them doesn't. The search terms don't adapt. The scoring criteria don't evolve. The thesis stays frozen in whatever slide deck launched the initiative.

What if the scouting system itself could learn from every decision — getting more precise with each thumbs-up, each thumbs-down, each "close but not quite"?

That's what Bene Gesserit does. It's a recursive intelligence system where every human judgment makes the next search sharper, the next score more accurate, and the thesis more refined. The system doesn't just find startups — it learns what "good" means to you, and it keeps learning.

02 — The Architecture

Six phases, strict dependency order

Bene Gesserit runs as a single orchestrated pipeline where each phase's output feeds the next. Nothing runs out of order. The system can be triggered manually or on a schedule — either way, the phases execute in strict sequence, ensuring the radar never runs with stale search terms and the thesis never updates without the latest vote data.

The full pipeline. Six phases in strict order, closing the loop through human judgment.

The human reviews the published results, votes, leaves feedback. The next cycle starts with those fresh signals. The loop closes — and the system converges.

03 — The Living Thesis

Strategy as a document that evolves

At the centre of the system is thesis.md — a living strategic document that defines what the system is searching for, why, and what "good" looks like. It's not a static brief. It's the system's understanding of the investment thesis, updated automatically based on what the human decision-maker reinforces or rejects.

The thesis contains the strategic vision (what kind of companies matter and why), a gap map (the specific technology categories being scouted), quality criteria (stage, geography, team, architecture preferences), learned preferences (patterns derived from voting data), and signals that challenge the thesis (competitive moves, regulatory shifts, market events that could invalidate assumptions).

Key insight

The thesis is both the input and the output of the system. It drives what gets searched and how results are scored — but it also absorbs every new signal and human judgment, meaning the strategy itself improves recursively. The thesis you write on day one will be unrecognisable by month three, shaped by hundreds of micro-decisions you've made along the way.

Version control is built in. The thesis carries a version log showing what changed and why — so you can trace the evolution of your strategic thinking over time.

05 — The Learning System

Every vote teaches the model something

This is the heart of what makes Bene Gesserit different from a search tool. The system maintains three learning mechanisms that turn human judgments into scoring improvements.

Preference learning

Every vote (1-5 stars) updates a preferences.md file — a structured record of what the system has learned about the decision-maker's tastes. Upvotes generate positive signals with point bonuses. Downvotes generate negative signals with point penalties. Neutral votes are tracked as ambiguous signals requiring further calibration. The preferences file feeds directly into the scoring algorithm — startups in subsequent scans are scored against these learned preferences, not just against the static thesis.

Exposure normalisation

Raw vote counts are misleading. If the radar surfaces 20 compliance startups and 4 agent-auth startups, and 3 get upvoted in each category, the naive signal is "equal interest." But the real signal is a 15% hit rate for compliance vs. 75% for agent-auth — a five-to-one preference that raw counts completely obscure.

The learning system normalises all preference signals by exposure — the number of startups surfaced per category, not just the number voted on. Categories are ranked by hit rate (upvotes ÷ exposures) and rejection rate (downvotes ÷ exposures), not by absolute volume. This prevents high-volume categories from drowning out genuine preferences in low-volume ones.

Thesis anchoring

Exposure normalisation solves the volume problem but introduces a new risk: the system could still drift away from the stated investment thesis if vote patterns happen to diverge from strategic intent. The thesis anchoring mechanism prevents this.

After computing normalised preferences, the system cross-references the results against the thesis priority map. Three outcomes are possible. Aligned: vote signal confirms a thesis priority — the system boosts confidence. Divergent: a non-thesis category is rising in the rankings — the system flags it as "volume-driven, not yet thesis-confirmed" and holds the score weight steady until the decision-maker explicitly confirms it. Data-starved: a thesis priority has little or no vote data — the system flags it as needing votes rather than letting it silently lose rank.

The net effect: the system learns from behaviour but doesn't overwrite strategy. Only an explicit human signal — a direct vote pattern with high hit rate and supporting vote notes, or a thesis feedback override — can shift the scoring weights away from the stated thesis.

Near-miss analysis

The most informative data points aren't the obvious hits or obvious misses — they're the near misses. A near miss is a startup that scored above 60 on the relevance model but received a downvote (2 stars or below). These are the cases where the system was confidently wrong, and they reveal what the model is getting wrong.

Example near miss

Stacks scored 82 (Agent Orchestration category) but received 2 stars. The human note: "Not really looking for accounting services. This is too small." The model had classified Stacks as a strong orchestration match without detecting that its primary product was accounting automation — bookkeeping and accounts payable — rather than financial services agentic infrastructure. The scoring model learned a new distinction: within Agent Orchestration, "finserv agentic workflow infrastructure" is preferred; "accounting/bookkeeping automation" is not.

Blind spot tracking

Near misses are categorised into blind spot types. When the same blind spot appears three times, the system proposes a formal scoring adjustment — a new soft factor with a specific point penalty or bonus, supported by the evidence that triggered it. This threshold prevents over-fitting to individual judgments while ensuring genuine patterns are captured.

Near-miss analysis pipeline. Confident errors are the highest-value learning data.

The learning system also tracks thesis drift — weekly observations about how the strategic landscape is changing, which gaps are generating signal vs. noise, and which emerging themes might warrant new gap categories. These observations are logged but not applied to scoring until confirmed by vote data. The system distinguishes between intelligence it has gathered and intelligence the human has validated.

06 — The Pipeline

From signal to scored card

When the Radar Agent finds a startup that passes the relevance threshold, it generates a structured intelligence card. Each card contains the company name, a one-line description, funding stage and amount, investor names, geography, the gap category it maps to, a relevance score (0-100), and the specific signals that triggered the match.

Cards are published to a web-based pipeline interface where the decision-maker can browse, search, filter, vote (1-5 stars), leave notes, flag for follow-up, or archive. The interface groups companies by gap category and sorts by score, giving an instant view of where the strongest candidates sit across the thesis. Keyword search runs across every visible card field — name, gap, geo, FinServ signal, team, and the "Why this matters" thesis — and ANDs cleanly with the state and team filters so a query like RIA seed narrows to exactly the intersection.

The scoring algorithm combines gap fit (does this company address a thesis gap?), stage match (is it at the right funding stage?), geographic fit, financial services readiness (evidence of regulated-industry traction), team signal (domain expertise, prior exits), and all learned preferences from the voting data. Each factor has a weight, and those weights shift as more votes accumulate — the system starts with reasonable defaults and converges on the decision-maker's actual priorities through use.

Importantly, each scan also produces a discovery log — an append-only record of what the system learned that week. Which search terms produced noise. Which gaps are generating hits vs. silence. What regulatory or competitive signals emerged. What changed in the market that might challenge the thesis. This log becomes a knowledge asset — a time-stamped history of how the landscape evolved.

07 — The Feedback Loop

Three channels of human input

The system accepts human judgment through three channels, each feeding a different part of the loop.

1. Votes

Star ratings (1-5) on individual startup cards. These are the primary calibration signal. An upvote teaches the system what a good match looks like. A downvote teaches what to avoid. A neutral vote reveals ambiguity that needs resolution. The vote note is as important as the stars — it captures the why, which the preference learning engine uses to extract sub-domain distinctions the scoring model can't derive from the rating alone.

2. Strategic feedback

A feedback form attached to the pipeline where the decision-maker can give high-level strategic commentary: "The thesis needs to focus more on X," or "Stop surfacing companies in category Y," or "The search terms for Z are too broad." This feeds directly into the Strategy Agent's thesis review.

3. Implicit signals

Which cards get opened, which get archived without reading, which trigger follow-up flags. These signals don't carry the weight of an explicit vote, but they're tracked as weak preferences that can be promoted to formal signals if patterns emerge.

The system treats the human decision-maker as the ground truth. Every other agent in the loop exists to serve, interpret, and act on that truth more effectively over time.

09 — What It Learned

From 8 votes, the system extracted this

Bene Gesserit has been running for five weeks. In that time, the decision-maker cast 8 votes — 4 upvotes, 2 downvotes, 2 neutrals. From those 8 data points, the system extracted a surprising amount of signal.

Strong positive
Agent Authentication & AI Safety have a 100% upvote rate (4/4 at 4-5 stars). The system learned that the decision-maker's strongest preference is for infrastructure-layer trust technology — products that other agents connect to, not products that use agents to do tasks.
Sub-domain precision
Both downvotes revealed the same pattern: right category, wrong product type. "Agent Orchestration" matched an accounting automation tool. "Tax Optimisation" matched a tax-filing app. The system learned to distinguish sub-domains within gaps — and added negative filters to prevent re-surfacing these types.
Dual failure modes
Two neutral votes in the Compliance category revealed two distinct failure paths: wrong sub-domain (KYC/AML framing when the preference is AI governance) and undifferentiated execution (correct framing but no architectural moat). The system now tracks these as separate blind spots.
Scope expansion
The system started with 6 gap categories and 50 search queries. As of mid-2026 it covers 22 gap categories and 336 search queries — the search surface tracking the thesis as it expands. The 2026-04-29 thesis bump to v2.7 added a three-team architecture (Research / Studio / Greens) with its own vocabulary blocks, including the Investable Everything framework (Product Inversion + Tokenization) and Wealth-Generation Reinvention.
Self-awareness
The system tracks its own data starvation. The persistent config/search-state.json file records per-team coverage, per-sub-area card counts, and "next-cycle targets" — empty cells the next sweep must fill. The negative-space audit runs at the end of every cycle and outputs a target list rather than letting blind spots silently persist.

This is the recursive learning flywheel in action. Each human judgment produces dozens of scoring adjustments, search term refinements, negative filters, blind spot trackers, and thesis drift observations — each of which makes the next scan more precise. The system doesn't need thousands of data points because it treats each vote as a high-value signal and extracts maximum information from it.

09b — Calibration Discipline

Hard gates, alignment audits, and post-mortems

A recursive system can drift in both directions. The thesis can over-constrain ("we only buy seed-stage Europe"). It can also under-constrain — get rationalised, gate-widened, scope-crept. Bene Gesserit's calibration discipline addresses both with deterministic checks rather than vibes.

09c — Privacy & Governance

A deterministic pre-publish gate

Bene Gesserit ingests private materials — uploaded screenshots, internal hypothesis notes, partner contact lists — and produces a public website at GitHub Pages. The default is conservative: anything shared with the system is private unless explicitly cleared. The structural protection is a deterministic pre-publish gate, not a manual review.

The scripts/privacy-check.sh script greps the working tree against config/redact-list.txt using word-bounded regex. If any redacted term appears in a file that would be pushed to GitHub, the check exits non-zero and prints all hits with file paths and line numbers. The redact list itself is gitignored — never reaches GitHub. Operators add terms to the list; the check enforces them.

What's redacted by default

Personal names from private materials (internal team members, partner contacts whose names came from uploaded notes); internal initiative names; sensitive partner identifiers. Kept: factual public-record references (founder names from press coverage of pipeline startups), reference archetypes (Kalshi, Polymarket, Robinhood), and external partner companies that have public BG presence (Mistral, Anthropic, Mantic).

The pre-publish discipline is enforced by hard rule in CLAUDE.md: every push must run scripts/privacy-check.sh first. A failed check blocks publication until terms are redacted and the check re-runs clean.

10 — Why It Works

The compounding advantage

Traditional scouting is linear: more effort in, proportionally more results out. Bene Gesserit is compounding: every cycle makes every future cycle more effective. The thesis sharpens. The search terms focus. The scoring model calibrates. The noise floor drops. The hit rate rises.

After five weeks, the system finds companies in categories it didn't know existed when it started. It rejects companies it would have surfaced in week one. It asks for specific votes on specific companies because it knows which data points would resolve specific ambiguities in its model. It writes its own weekly intelligence briefings that track market shifts the decision-maker hasn't had time to monitor.

The key insight is that startup scouting is a preference learning problem, not a search problem. Finding startups is easy — the internet is full of them. The hard part is knowing which ones matter to you, for your thesis, at this moment. That's a problem that only gets solved by a system that watches what you do, learns from it, and adapts.

The best search tool isn't the one with the most data. It's the one that knows what you're actually looking for — and keeps getting closer.
